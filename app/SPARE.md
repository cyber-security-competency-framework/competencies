# Cyber Resilience
####Detect, respond to, and learn from issues and incidents and anticipate attacks.#### Patrol the tech stack, sniff out anomalies, manage incident response and continuously improve through learning from issues, incidents and external threat intelligence. When attackers try to infiltrate, resilience means detecting, investigating, containing and recovering from their attacks. Cyber resilience competencies overlap with hardening competencies when deploying sensors and collecting logs. These competencies include building and operating automated detection systems and automated and semi-automated response playbooks to make sure that, day in and day out, systems remain secure.

## Security Monitoring <!-- tooltip: Monitoring networks, systems and applications for anomalous behaviour and policy violations to detect and provide context to enrich alerting on potential information security issues and incidents -->

### Novice
- Can explain the need for system and service telemetry to be observed, monitored and retained for security purposes and can list the operational practices, log types and technology involved in achieving this.
- Can explain the main principles and tools used in the collection, processing and analysis of data relevant to the detection and investigation of security events.
- Can review logs and recognise obvious anomalies. 

### Advanced Beginner
- Uses basic SIEM tools (e.g. viewing alerts, dashboards). 
- Contributes to feedback processes and mechanisms for continuous improvement of security monitoring and incident response.
- Able to identify suspicious software, including potential malware sources.

### Practitioner
- Develops feedback processes and mechanisms for continuous improvement of security monitoring and incident response. 
- Develops and tests rules for detecting attacks and conditions exceeding security limits under supervision.
- Configures detections with indicators of compromise on infrastructure, malicious software and any associated Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs).
- Configures data sources and security monitoring tools (e.g. SIEM, EDR, NDR). 

### Proficient
- Designs and optimises detection pipelines (ETL/ELT pipelines and data models).
- Integrates monitoring into system design (e.g. observability and detection as code). 
- Is a subject matter expert in developing and operationalising techniques for security detection processes, e.g. detecting anomalous activity, analytics to reduce false positives and increase true positives, automating detection correlation, triage and prioritisation.

### Expert
- Leads enterprise cyber attack detection strategy and collaborates with other relevant disciplines such as fraud and physical security. 
- Develops novel detection engineering practices e.g. detection and triage automation, graph-based architecture, agentic security operations. 
- Collaborates across security operations, engineering teams and control owners to build proactive and automated defence capabilities.

## Incident & Issue Response <!-- tooltip: Prepare, practice and execute quick, effective, consistent and orderly response to information security issues (vulnerabilities, misconfigurations and other weaknesses) and incidents (attacks and urgent policy violations), including communication on security events. -->

### Novice
- Can describe the basic principles of issue discovery and management, incident management, incident investigation and response. 
- Can implement basic, templated incident response plans and follow it when an incident is detected. 
- Understands the differences between issue management, incident response and forensic evidential requirements.

### Advanced Beginner
- Can determine the severity of an issue or incident and get the necessary people or teams involved efficiently and promptly, providing the information they need as they need it. 
- Contributes to feedback mechanisms for continuous improvement of issue management, incident response, recovery, prevention measures and the information security management system.
- Analyses digital evidence to derive information required to help resolve security incidents, amplify weaknesses and/or identify breaches of policy, regulation or law.

### Practitioner
- Can make strategic adjustments to standardised incident response plans and orchestrate the relevant communications and remediations necessary in the event of an incident.
- Develops feedback mechanisms for continuous improvement of issue management, incident response, recovery, preventative measures and the information security management system.
- Analyses technically complex digital evidence and investigates complicated computer security incidents to derive information required to help resolve security incidents, weaknesses, and/or identify breaches of policy, regulation or law. 
- Adapts techniques, modifies tools and creates scripts to address atypical investigations and remediation. 
- Addresses analysis and forensic requirements arising from cloud and distributed environments, agentic AI and emerging technologies. 

### Proficient
- Takes the lead in forming a Security Incident Response Team (SIRT) or weakness management programme, quickly gets the relevant parties involved, defines policy and takes the lead in communicating ongoing work, business impact and recommendations for improvement to stakeholders. 
- Sets direction within the organisation for all aspects of investigation, forensic and containment activities.  
- Reverse engineers malware and enumerates attack paths to further investigative and intelligence opportunities. 
- Provides clear explanations of impact and recommendations to senior stakeholders (including the highest levels of management), and detailed explanations to technical specialists.

### Expert
- Proactively prepares teams for incident response through simulations. 
- Uses deep expertise, AI and novel technology to enhance Security Incident Response Team (SIRT) or weakness management, perform deep root cause analysis, identify, radiate and propose preventative measures for repeat causes of security events. 
- Analyses technically complex digital evidence and investigates highly complicated and novel computer security incidents to derive information required to help resolve security incidents, weaknesses and/or identify breaches of policy, regulation or law.

## Threat and Internal Context <!-- tooltip: Learn from incidents, articulate the stages and components of attacks and map to the business and technology context of the organisation to inform decision-making and prioritise defensive measures for attacker tactics and techniques. -->

### Novice
- Understands fundamentals of how organisations can be attacked - can describe the cyber kill chain, MITRE ATT&CK tactics and similar.
- Recognises basic attack techniques (e.g. SQL Injection, Cross-Site Scripting).
- Can describe asset inventory requirements, operations and tactics.

### Advanced Beginner
- Understands at a surface level how security weaknesses are exploited. 
- Understands and can explain the intelligence cycle, threat assessment and threat modelling principles and concepts. 
- Uses prescribed tools and techniques to acquire, validate and analyse threat, asset and important business services information from multiple sources. 
- Under direction enriches threat information by providing business and asset context, assessing possible implications for the organisation and summarising the behaviour, capabilities and activities of threat actors. 
- Applies knowledge to enrich security monitoring, incident response, issue management, risk management or security engineering (e.g. threat modelling) processes. 

### Practitioner
- Diagnoses attack paths through misconfigurations and vulnerabilities in systems, processes or code. 
- Ensures outputs from security team include context, improve decision-making and where available, recommended fix and treatment options. 
- Identifies new sources of threat, asset and business context and utilises a variety of techniques, without supervision, to acquire, validate and analyse threat and asset information, enterprise attack vectors, and critical organisational functions from multiple sources. 
- Develops processes to enrich and disseminate threat information, including understanding the behaviour, capabilities and activities of threat actors, assessing possible implications by synthesising and placing intelligence in the asset and business context and prioritising remediation of identified weaknesses for systems and processes. 

### Proficient
- Understands exploitation techniques in depth (e.g. memory corruption, privilege escalation). 
- Develops new products and services to prioritise and scope security testing, system hardening, detection engineering and service recovery.
- Synthesises and places complex intelligence in context, understanding relevance in the context of organisational architecture and strategy. 
- Leads and oversees the threat intelligence function and business context activities for the organisation.
- Participates in sector-level intelligence sharing initiatives.

### Expert
- Proposes and helps to develop innovative defence techniques against emerging threats.
- Leads sector or national-level intelligence sharing initiatives.
- Designs and delivers new threat intelligence products and services for stakeholders outside the organisation such as threat intelligence-based red teaming.

# System Hardening
####System level building and testing to prevent, find, and fix exploitable weaknesses and to make attacks less likely, less impactful and more recoverable.#### System hardening architecture and engineering, ensuring that access, data, code, infrastructure are designed to resist attacks. This is about building and testing data, software and the infrastructure hosting and accessing the data and software so they are verifiably secure. It is the essential task of developing and verifying secure-by-default and secure-by-design infrastructure and code (and infrastructure as code). Competencies include securing cloud, CI/CD and the code, seamlessly weaving security into the fabric of the software development lifecycle across buildtime and runtime.

## Product Security <!-- tooltip: Implementation of secure systems, products and components, using appropriate methodologies and frameworks. It includes integrating security test automation and controls into the development process, within a platform-driven environment. This involves making sure that automated testing and controls can be scaled and are reliable across older (legacy) and newer (modern) systems. -->

### Novice
- Understands and can describe a basic software/service development life cycle. 
- Can describe common AppSec vulnerabilities and standards (i.e. OWASP Top 10 and ASVS).
- Understands Infrastructure as Code (IaC), and can deploy basic templates with supervision (e.g. Terraform, CloudFormation).
- Understands basic secure by default and design (SbDD) concepts and collaborates with software developers on simple scenarios.
- Executes tests in continuous integration pipelines with guidance. 
- Follows predefined security assurance standards for individual tasks.

### Advanced Beginner
- Writes SbDD scenarios for individual features and configures continuous testing workflows. 
- Works with cross-functional teams and QAs to align testing efforts across more than one product or service. 
- Contributes to the improvement of security assurance standards for team workflows.
- Adopts lean principles to minimise redundant testing and focus on value delivery.
- Designs and executes test cases, and configures basic security test automation frameworks and tooling for code, CI/CD and cloud.

### Practitioner
- Embeds SbDD into cross-functional workflows, facilitating collaboration between Security, QA, and Development to ensure security is a native feature of the software lifecycle.
- Implements continuous security testing strategies across all environments (Dev to Prod) that align with lean principles, platform scalability, and legacy modernisation efforts.
- Audits and secures IaC templates and cloud environments (AWS, Azure, GCP), integrating automated security scanning into pipelines to prevent misconfiguration.
- Configures and manages CI/CD security integrations and Cloud Security Posture Management (CSPM) tools to provide actionable visibility for engineering and monitoring teams.
- Secures the software supply chain by deploying pre-assured components, auditing third-party libraries and components, tools to identify and remediate upstream vulnerabilities.
- Design and implement tooling to autonomously discover and fix weaknesses within system components while maintaining context of how those components interact with the wider system architecture.

### Proficient
- Scales SbDD practices across teams, and develops monitoring systems to report improvements and gaps in practice coverage and adoption.
- Aligns continuous testing efforts with organisational modernisation initiatives. 
- Leads efforts to establish security assurance standards across cross-functional teams, focusing on consistency, scalability, reduced waste and optimising flow.
- Champions secure coding practices, including guardrails for both experienced developers and vibe-coding. 
- Defines secure library and IaC patterns, automates policy enforcement and enables teams for secure provisioning.

### Expert
- Leads the enterprise adoption of SbDD, driving shared understanding and continuous collaboration. 
- Shapes security assurance collaboration frameworks to remove silos and enable continuous improvement. 
- Shapes continuous testing frameworks that embed security testing into every stage of development. 
- Shapes enterprise security assurance standards, embedding continuous improvement and system-wide alignment. 
- Drives cultural adoption of security testing and control loops, ensuring alignment with agile and platform-driven development.

## Security Architecture <!-- tooltip: The secure design of computer systems. It combines technical architecture and risk management, along with knowledge of how systems can be compromised to help design systems that (among other things) are sufficiently hard to compromise or disrupt while being sufficiently easy to use, monitor and maintain. -->

### Novice
- Can describe the basic architecture and core ideas of major security frameworks and standards including NIST RMF & CSF, ISO27001/2, NCSC Cybersecurity Assessment Framework and Center for Internet Security Controls. 
- Aware of basic security services to protect on premises and cloud-native systems. 
- Can describe basic authentication mechanisms. 
- Understands that threat modelling identifies system weaknesses and can follow and interpret existing threat models.

### Advanced Beginner
- Can specify basic security services required to protect against common security threats with minimal help
- Demonstrates knowledge of internal and external sources of published security architecture guidance, including secure design principles and patterns. 
- Demonstrates broad-ranging technical security knowledge necessary to understand system architectures, including common server roles, cryptography, key management, identity and access control, security technologies, virtual private networks (VPNs), load balancers, Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS) and Software as a Service (SaaS). 
- Needs support to review security architecture before the system is exposed to real risk on the Internet.

### Practitioner
- Able to architect and specify required security services taking into account the key tradeoffs and cybersecurity risks a customer has articulated. 
- Aware of and able to navigate potential tradeoffs between security and other architectural concerns such as accessibility and decentralisation.
- Independently creates threat models for moderately complex systems and prioritises threat mitigations based on threat intelligence.

### Proficient 
- Can architect enterprise model architectures for systems to have the ability to protect, detect, respond and recover from security threats.
- Able to overlay data protection and privacy goals of the system into a technical plan for data at rest.
- Proactively works with enterprise risk teams to identify a system's risk profile and compliance requirements and incorporates these into the design.
- Leads cross-functional threat modelling workshops and identifies subtle, multi-stage threat vectors and defines layered mitigations.

### Expert
- Can make recommendations, including to senior stakeholders, on trade-offs that appear in designing enterprise-level security systems.
- Able to innovate with more efficient and effective ways to protect, detect, respond and recover from security threats.
- Able to use the experience to teach and coach others in security architecture. 
- Defines threat modelling frameworks for the organisation, trains others in advanced threat modelling practices and connects threat modelling to organisational risk and strategy.

## Identity and Access <!-- tooltip: The policies, engineering and operational practices and methodologies that seek to mitigate the risk of unauthorised access and  exploiting legitimate access to the organisation's assets. -->
### Novice
- Can describe the basic principles of Identity & Access Management (IAM), Identity Governance and Administration (IGA) and Privileged Access Management (PAM) controls through relevant references in frameworks such as ISO27002, NIST CSF, CIS and NCSC CAF. 
- Knowledge of basic principles, such as Principle of Least Privilege, authentication, authorisation, audit and observability.

### Advanced Beginner
- Can explain how the principles of IAM apply within the organisation, referencing the relevant frameworks and security controls.
- Knowledge of common misconfigurations and good practices in areas such as access control, secrets management, toxic combinations, access governance, privilege access management, and monitoring & logging.


### Practitioner
- Understands organisation and service IAM policies and processes and the operation of identity platforms (including identities created in SaaS and by Agentic AI).
- Undertakes tasks relating to IAM and Privilege Access Management (PAM) under supervision.
- Recognises and addresses misconfigurations and non-compliance, makes recommendations for change and prevents future recurrence.
- Can provision security services such as secrets management, identity authentication, authorisation and audit, attack path hardening, privilege segmentation or security monitoring.

### Proficient
- Leads the development, revision, implementation or monitoring of internal IAM policies and processes within an organisation.
- Performs tasks relating to IAM without human supervision, including routine management of IAM and PAM systems.
- Can advocate for and help implement practices such as Role-based access control (RBAC) or attribute-based access control (ABAC) and access policy-as-code.

### Expert
- Leads or provides SME to the development, revision, implementation or monitoring of internal and customer IAM policies, processes and related tooling across the organisation.
- Undertakes complex tasks relating to IAM and PAM with no supervision, including the implementation and configuration of IAM or PAM systems.
- Acts as designated identity security SME to develop policy and influence major strategic tech decisions, help implement those solutions, and define enterprise identity and access management and governance strategy.

## Core Infrastructure Security <!-- tooltip: Discovering and amplifying security weaknesses in third-party built, enterprise-configured networks, servers, devices and applications through vulnerability and posture management scanning and automated and manual testing. -->

### Novice
- Can explain the business impacts security weaknesses can have and the challenges of finding, fixing and preventing them. 
- Can describe structured methods including weakness categorisation, scoring methods and public vulnerability databases. 
- Able to interpret the output from basic automated security scanners and put simple fixes in place.
- Understands and can explain the difference between passive and active scanning and penetration testing and their purpose, and recognises the value of scanning and testing in supporting information security and configuration compliance. 
- Aware that cloud services have specific security needs and understands basic cloud security concepts and practices.

### Advanced Beginner
- Can explain relative strengths of common vulnerability reporting, classification and scoring systems (e.g., CVE, CWE, CVSS, EPSS, OWASP Top 10).
- Able to discover and propose fixes for weaknesses by running tools.
- Uses cloud security tools (e.g. discovering vulnerabilities, misconfigurations and toxic combinations) to prioritise and communicate weaknesses to software and infrastructure engineers with guidance.
- Can describe the value of fixes to product owners in general terms. 
- Understands the basic principles, processes and components of penetration testing and can describe how these are applied in practice. 
- Appreciates the risks of testing and the governance associated in executing such tests.	

### Practitioner
- Can configure scanners and testing platforms for a wide range of vulnerabilities and misconfigurations in modern software and infrastructure stacks. 
- Able to systematically manually test APIs and applications for weaknesses in validation or authorisation.
- Able to replicate and contextualise vulnerabilities found by automated or manual exploratory testing according to business value.
- Able to diagnose and describe issues with false positives from testing and scanning tooling and configure pipelines to avoid 'alert fatigue'.
- Explains the principles of penetration testing, the main components of an infrastructure penetration test and the high-level processes involved, to practitioners and non-practitioners alike.
- Provides pragmatic input to assist in the development of penetration testing policies, procedures and guidelines and understands their business context.

### Proficient
- Stays up to date with industry intelligence on common and emerging vulnerabilities and other weakness exploitation.
- Can teach others how to effectively use automated tooling and how to get started with manual vulnerability testing.
- Designs and implements passive and active weakness discovery solutions for mid-complexity systems, products, applications or processes, selecting suitable techniques, tools and test strategies without supervision.
- Creates assessment reports, confirming technology compliance with standards and policies and vulnerabilities, and provides suggested remediation actions.

### Expert
- Leads and implements data, application and cloud vulnerability and misconfiguration posture management and workload protection services backed by tooling, capability investments and metrics.
- Able to perform assessments of software systems and secure delivery lifecycles, providing prioritised findings and recommendations to senior business and risk management stakeholders.
- Can assure the soundness of security enforcing functions of systems exhibiting high degrees of cybersecurity sensitivity.
- Leads weakness discovery services and drives organisational and business change to wire weakness discovery, remediation and prevention into processes. 
- Leads continuous improvement of compliance including policy, procedure, guideline and monitoring improvements.

## Data Security <!-- tooltip: Multi-disciplinary structures, policies, procedures, processes and technical controls to manage the protection of important data, including supporting immediate and future regulatory, legal, risk, and operational requirements. -->

### Novice
- Understands the importance of data classification and can explain why different types of data require different levels of protection.
- Familiar with foundational principles of secure information handling, including need-to-know, data minimisation, and purpose limitation.
- Aware of key data privacy regulations (e.g. GDPR, DPA) and the basic obligations they impose on organisations.
- Can describe common data security risks such as data leakage, unauthorised access, and insecure data disposal.

### Advanced Beginner
- Can classify and handle information in line with organisational policy.
- Understands common data protection controls including encryption at rest and in transit, access controls, and data masking.
- Can identify situations where data usage raises privacy or compliance concerns and escalates appropriately.
- Familiar with practical implications of data protection regulations, such as data subject rights and breach notification requirements.

### Practitioner
- Knows the relevant risks, laws, regulations and organisational obligations relating to the data they work with.
- Implements data security controls including DLP tooling, encryption, data masking, and secure data lifecycle management.
- Conducts data discovery and classification exercises to identify and protect sensitive data assets.
- Advises teams on secure data handling practices and privacy-by-design principles.
- Identifies and escalates data security risks and privacy compliance gaps.

### Proficient
- Acts as a champion for responsible data handling, advising and informing others across the organisation.
- Designs and implements data security architectures including classification schemes, DLP programmes, and data lifecycle controls.
- Implements data anonymisation, pseudonymisation, and redaction techniques to protect sensitive information at scale.
- Leads on data breach response activities, including regulatory notification obligations and stakeholder communications.
- Defines frameworks for data security testing and monitoring, including automated pipelines for continuous compliance assurance.

### Expert
- Responsible for enterprise data security strategy, setting direction for data classification, protection, and governance programmes.
- Identifies and evaluates complex data security risks across the organisation, including cross-border data flows and third-party data sharing.
- Leads regulatory engagement relating to data protection and acts as the organisation's expert on evolving data privacy law.
- Defines and continuously improves the organisation's data security management system, including policies, standards, and metrics.
- Influences senior stakeholders on data security investment and risk tolerance decisions.

# Secure the Organisation
####Security at scale, implementing control loops, process improvements and amplifying individual contribution with workflows and automation, ensuring that the organisation is not only secure but also adaptable, efficient and resilient.#### Scales security practices, success measures and compliance assessment to enterprise level through policies, process, playbooks, automation and controls adherence monitoring . Ensures that each team is playing fairly, within the bounds of the organisation's security policies and standards, adjusting those policies and standards to fit the business and threat context. Operational focus on automating security playbooks, protocols and procedures to accelerate time to fix and keep systems up to date. Like systems engineers, they don’t just apply security - they automate it, ensuring that security processes are fast, reliable, and scalable. They work closely with development and operations to create pipelines where every finding, fix, patch, update, metric or evidence of compliance is transmitted and reacted to automatically (or as automatically as possible).

## Governance, Risk and Compliance <!-- tooltip: Monitors and evaluates security control coverage, risks to information, systems, and processes owned by the organisation, proactively refines policies and standards and ensures continuous improvement of the information security management system. -->

### Novice
- Can articulate risk as a value concept. Understands the importance of evaluating security requirements for any particular system. 
- Understands basic cybersecurity principles (e.g. confidentiality, integrity, availability, authenticity, non-repudiation, distributed, immutable, ephemeral etc). 
- Can run a risk assessment process but lacks practical management knowledge. 
- Aware that there are legal and regulatory requirements (e.g., GDPR, NIS).	

### Advanced Beginner
- Can describe typical cybersecurity risks relevant to a system and the common practises and requirements in order to meet them. 
- Can act as an active participant in requirements focused on the risk profile of the system or threat modelling sessions. 
- Follows compliance-related processes and understands and can communicate why certain security controls are mandatory.
- Can describe and provide examples of Policy as Code.

### Practitioner
- Can articulate the importance of addressing cybersecurity risk to stakeholders. 
- Can lead risk modelling sessions to determine financial and other consequences of technical threats, vulnerabilities and missing technical controls in a particular system. 
- Can facilitate sessions to help determine the risk profile of the system, based on the types of information processed and the impact if control of that information is lost. 
- Verifies systems and processes meet compliance standards and supports audits and documentation efforts.

### Proficient
- Actively reaches out to data, system and service owners, central functions and enterprise risk teams to understand the broader organisational perspective on cybersecurity risk. 
- Advocates and co-ordinates security as a continuous process to address the balance of risk. 
- Can escalate and convene with broader stakeholders when outcomes from controls monitoring and risk profiling sessions point to solutions which are broader in scope than the team. 
- Designs systems and security programs with compliance in mind and maps security controls to multiple regulatory frameworks.

### Expert
- Experienced in transforming organisations to better address cybersecurity and risk. 
- Able to connect risk registers with real investment and systems of work to identify and improve the risk balance of a particular system. 
- Able to coach and train others in identifying and delivering on security risk and regulatory requirements.
- Acts as a thought leader on connecting security teams to wider issues of risk. 
- Defines compliance strategies and advises executives and legal teams on complex regulatory environments.
- Leads solution design and delivery of enterprise compliance initiatives (e.g. ISO 27001 certification, SOC2, PCI-DSS and Cybersecurity Assessment Framework).

## Security Process Engineering <!-- tooltip: Incorporate human factors, automation and AI into the  design of security controls, control loops and workflows. Improve the quality and efficiency of controls and control loops by creating security products and experiences that overcome common judgement errors and support clear thinking. -->

### Novice
- Understands the basic theory that how data is presented affects interpretation. 
- Can list the benefits of clear data visualisation, narratives and well-designed communications for reducing misinterpretation and driving positive change.
- Can explain basic concepts, such as the difference between correlation and causation, and why simplicity in a dashboard is important.
- Recognises the role of cyber and information security awareness and training and can list the benefits of behavioural analysis and security culture management in maintaining good cyber and information security.
- Writes simple scripts, modifies existing code (e.g., Python, Bash) or deploys simple processes in low code automation tooling. 

### Advanced Beginner
- Applies basic principles under guidance to improve the clarity of security training, user experience and reporting products.
- Contributes to the development of training, reports or dashboards by applying established best practices for communication and data visualisation.
- Supports the team by identifying potential areas of user confusion in existing security data and tools.
- Can apply simple "nudges" in training, communications and report design, such as using colour to draw attention to a key metric or setting a sensible default view.
- Writes policy as code, small programs or scripts for automating basic tasks (e.g., log parsing, simple reports) or automates security processes with more than one decision criteria in low code automation tooling.

### Practitioner
- Designs and builds decision-support and training tools that actively account for human factors.
- Designs and develops intuitive communications, dashboards and reports that guide users toward key insights and minimise cognitive load.
- Analyses user interaction data to identify where decision-makers struggle and proposes concrete design improvements.
- Proactively reframes security questions or metrics to mitigate common biases, such as presenting data in terms of both gains and losses to avoid framing effects.
- Contributes to human-led, deterministic and agentic workflow design decisions applying criteria such as predictability of the task, the tolerance for risk/error, context or creativity required and need for scale and speed.
- Develops tooling to automate security tasks (e.g. triaging, routing and tracking findings from vulnerability scanners, alert handlers, compliance monitoring and evidence collection). 
- Publishes automation processes or AI tooling to code repositories or an app store.
- Builds and maintains ETL/ELT pipelines and data models within a data store.

### Proficient
- Strategically designs security training, communications and decision-making experiences and measures their impact.
- Uses statistical analysis and human-centered design principles to build and test different ways of presenting information (e.g. running A/B tests on dashboard designs to see which one leads to better, faster decisions).
- Removes bias from critical security processes, such as strategic planning, forecasting and reporting, by embedding data-driven checks and balances.
- Acts as a subject matter expert, advising security teams on how to structure their data and analysis to support optimal communication and decision-making.
- Leads human-led, deterministic and agentic workflow design and implements repeatable and structured criteria within a design framework for others to use.
- Designs and implements robust automation and AI augmentation frameworks and tools (e.g. for threat detection, security finding and incident remediation and risk operations). 
- Automates security playbooks, policies and guardrails in workflows, CI/CD and/or on endpoints (including devices, browsers and IDEs).

### Expert
- Shapes the organisation's overall strategy for behaviour change and security decision making and fosters a culture of data-informed critical thinking.
- Builds and leads enterprise-wide programs that embed security principles into the company's core infrastructure and workflows.
- Drives cultural change by persuading senior leadership of the measurable value of investing in security decision intelligence.
- Pioneers new methods for evaluating and improving the quality of security decisions across the organisation, establishing a systemic framework for learning from both good and bad outcomes.
- Develops complex security automation or Agentic AI workflows.
